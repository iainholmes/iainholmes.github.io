Infrastructure projects generate complex spatial externalities that ripple through local housing markets. In this paper, I utilize cross-sectional data variation in dam locations across 747 Zip Code Tabulation Areas (ZCTAs) in North Carolina to estimate the capitalization of dam proximity into housing values.

Theoretical models suggest a non-linear tradeoff where reservoirs act as recreational amenities, while dam structures pose disamenity risks. Using a hedonic pricing model with quadratic functional forms, standard OLS estimates reveal modest relationships. However, after correcting for the endogeneity of residential sorting via an Instrumental Variable (IV) approach, I find a statistically significant non-linear relationship (p < 0.05).

The results indicate that dam proximity serves as a net amenity for approximately **22.3 miles** before the effect dissipates. These findings suggest that the amenity capitalization of water infrastructure is spatially extensive and robust to controls for structural quality and population density. For policymakers, this implies that the economic benefits of dam maintenance and reservoir preservation extend across broader tax bases than previously estimated.

**Key Methods:** Hedonic Pricing Model, Ordinary Least Squares (OLS), Two-Stage Least Squares / Control Function (IV), HC3 Robust Standard Errors, Geospatial Distance Calculation (Python/geopy)

**Data Sources:** U.S. Census Bureau (ACS 2023), National Inventory of Dams (USACE), NC Department of Environmental Quality, U.S. Census Gazetteer Files
