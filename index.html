About Me

I am a second-year graduate student at North Carolina State University pursuing a Master of Science in Economics with a dual concentration in Data Analytics and Natural Resource & Environmental Economics. My academic work sits at the intersection of rigorous quantitative analysis and real-world environmental challenges — examining how infrastructure, contamination, and policy shape economic behavior and housing markets across North Carolina.

Coursework spans Applied Econometrics I & II, Environmental Resource & Policy, Big Data Econometrics, and Applied Microeconometrics. In Spring 2026 I served as a Graduate Teaching Assistant for EC 490 (Senior Seminar) and EC 410 (Public Finance), grading assignments and mentoring undergraduates developing capstone research papers on market strategy, externalities, and regulation.

Alongside coursework I serve as a Research Assistant at Veritas Economic Consulting in Cary, NC, where I develop econometric models in Stata and Analytica, design stated-preference surveys eliciting willingness-to-pay for environmental programs, and contribute analysis supporting expert witness testimony on EPA Clean Water Act compliance matters. I also recently completed a contract role at Environmental Economics Services, LLC, working with Dr. Rick Dunford on a Natural Resource Damages textbook — validating empirical frameworks and updating regression models for prior NRD case studies.

My current thesis examines the Public's Willingness-to-Pay for proposed PFAS surface water regulations in North Carolina, using contingent valuation cross-validated with revealed-preference evidence from residential property markets. Broader interests include non-market valuation, spatial econometrics, identity economics, and the economic consequences of environmental contamination on communities.

Thesis & Research

Thesis · In Progress

Valuing Regulatory Justification: A Tipping Point Analysis of PFAS Surface Water Quality Standards in North Carolina

This research moves beyond aggregate cost-benefit metrics to identify the marginal cost per household at which proposed PFAS surface water regulations become welfare-improving to the public bearing the burden. While the state's Regulatory Impact Analysis (RIA) indicates aggregate monetized benefits of approximately $11.67 billion outweigh $11.19 billion in costs, these totals mask the friction between diffuse, long-term health benefits and the concentrated, near-term costs realized by ratepayers and industrial dischargers. Employing contingent valuation and choice experiments cross-validated with revealed preference evidence from residential property markets, this thesis elicits willingness-to-pay (WTP) thresholds for specific regulatory outcomes. The resulting marginal cost threshold provides a politically and economically justifiable price point directly actionable for the North Carolina Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ) in regulatory proceedings and public engagement.

Working Paper · ECG 561 Applied Econometrics I

A Dam Catch-22.3: The Non-Linear Spatial Effects of North Carolina's Water Infrastructure on Housing Markets

Infrastructure projects generate complex spatial externalities that ripple through local housing markets in ways traditional methods often fail to capture. Using cross-sectional data across 747 Zip Code Tabulation Areas (ZCTAs) in North Carolina and geolocated data for over 3,600 dams, I employ a hedonic pricing model with quadratic functional forms to estimate the capitalization of dam proximity into residential property values. Standard OLS estimates yield modest relationships, but after correcting for the endogeneity of residential sorting via an Instrumental Variable (IV) strategy, I identify a statistically significant non-linear amenity gradient (p < 0.05). Dams act as a net amenity for approximately 22.3 miles before the price effect dissipates — a finding that challenges the arbitrary fixed-radius assumptions common in infrastructure policy analysis and cost-benefit assessment.

Iain Holmes · NC State University, Department of Economics · December 2025

Econometric Advisor — A Research Design Tool for Applied Econometrics

The Econometric Advisor is a browser-based companion that helps applied researchers move from a research question to a defensible identification strategy. A short adaptive intake produces a tailored econometric playbook — recommended estimator, functional-form considerations, spatial diagnostics where relevant, and an inference checklist calibrated to the user's specific design. The full app is built in vanilla HTML and JavaScript with no backend, no build step, and no infrastructure dependencies. The intent is portability over polish — anyone with a browser and an idea for a study can use it, and anyone who wants to fork it has the entire system in a single file. Quantitative Practice Tool · Open Access

Quant Skills Trainer — A Multi-Language Curriculum for Applied Econometrics

The Quant Skills Trainer is a structured, browser-based practice environment for building applied econometric fluency across Python, R, and Stata. The curriculum moves from core data-handling tasks through panel methods, instrumental variables, and frontier causal-inference techniques — Difference-in-Differences, Regression Discontinuity, and Double Machine Learning — with identical module coverage across all three languages so users can learn new syntax against methods they already understand. Each module pairs a written lesson with a hands-on exercise designed to be completed in the user’s own local environment (Jupyter, RStudio, or Stata’s do-file editor), then submitted for AI-driven code review powered by a transparent, domain-specific evaluation rubric.
