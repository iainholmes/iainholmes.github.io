About Me

I am a second-year graduate student at North Carolina State University pursuing a Master of Science in Economics with a dual concentration in Data Analytics and Natural Resource & Environmental Economics. My work sits at the intersection of rigorous quantitative analysis and real-world environmental challenges — examining how infrastructure, contamination, and policy shape economic behavior and housing markets across North Carolina.

Alongside my coursework, I serve as a Research Assistant at Veritas Economic Consulting in Cary, NC, where I develop econometric models, design stated-preference surveys eliciting willingness-to-pay for environmental programs, and contribute analysis to expert witness proceedings on EPA compliance matters. I have also contributed to a Natural Resource Damages textbook while working with Dr. Rick Dunford at Environmental Economics Services, LLC. I assisted in validating empirical frameworks and ensuring statistical model accuracy across numerous prior cases regarding hazardous substance release and damages.

Prior to graduate school, I earned a B.A. in Economics with a Minor in Philosophy from the University of Virginia and built a career in financial services. That applied, analytical background informs the precision and rigor I bring to every research question I pursue.

I am passionate about leveraging econometric and statistical tools to inform policy, with a particular focus on the non-market valuation of environmental goods, spatial econometrics, and the economic consequences of environmental contamination on communities.

Thesis · In Progress

Valuing Regulatory Justification: A Tipping Point Analysis of PFAS Surface Water Quality Standards in North Carolina

This research moves beyond aggregate cost-benefit metrics to identify the marginal cost per household at which proposed PFAS surface water regulations become welfare-improving to the public bearing the burden. While the state's Regulatory Impact Analysis (RIA) indicates aggregate monetized benefits of approximately $11.67 billion outweigh $11.19 billion in costs, these totals mask the friction between diffuse, long-term health benefits and the concentrated, near-term costs realized by ratepayers and industrial dischargers. Employing contingent valuation and choice experiments cross-validated with revealed preference evidence from residential property markets, this thesis elicits willingness-to-pay (WTP) thresholds for specific regulatory outcomes. The resulting marginal cost threshold provides a politically and economically justifiable price point directly actionable for the North Carolina Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ) in regulatory proceedings and public engagement.

Working Paper · ECG 561 Applied Econometrics I

A Dam Catch-22.3: The Non-Linear Spatial Effects of North Carolina's Water Infrastructure on Housing Markets

Infrastructure projects generate complex spatial externalities that ripple through local housing markets in ways traditional methods often fail to capture. Using cross-sectional data across 747 Zip Code Tabulation Areas (ZCTAs) in North Carolina and geolocated data for over 3,600 dams, I employ a hedonic pricing model with quadratic functional forms to estimate the capitalization of dam proximity into residential property values. Standard OLS estimates yield modest relationships, but after correcting for the endogeneity of residential sorting via an Instrumental Variable (IV) strategy, I identify a statistically significant non-linear amenity gradient (p < 0.05). Dams act as a net amenity for approximately 22.3 miles before the price effect dissipates — a finding that challenges the arbitrary fixed-radius assumptions common in infrastructure policy analysis and cost-benefit assessment.

Research Tool · Open Access

Econometric Advisor — An AI-Powered Research Design Tool for Applied Economics

Launch app →

Designing a credible identification strategy is one of the most consequential — and underserved — decisions a researcher faces early in a project. This browser-based tool guides users through a structured twelve-question assessment of their research design, then generates a personalized econometric toolkit: an identification strategy, estimator recommendation, functional form guidance, spatial diagnostics, and an inference checklist, each calibrated to the specific study. When an Anthropic API key is provided, the tool additionally produces a plain-English narrative summary of the research design and a curated literature review drawn from relevant applied economics papers. Built in pure HTML and JavaScript and deployed on GitHub Pages, the Advisor reflects my interest in making rigorous econometric thinking accessible to graduate students and researchers navigating the identification landscape for the first time.
