About Me

I am a second-year graduate student at North Carolina State University pursuing a Master of Science in Economics with a dual concentration in Data Analytics and Natural Resource & Environmental Economics. My work sits at the intersection of rigorous quantitative analysis and real-world environmental challenges — examining how infrastructure, contamination, and policy shape economic behavior and housing markets across North Carolina.

Alongside my coursework, I serve as a Research Assistant at Veritas Economic Consulting in Cary, NC, where I develop econometric models, design stated-preference surveys eliciting willingness-to-pay for environmental programs, and contribute analysis to expert witness proceedings on EPA compliance matters. I also contributed to a Natural Resource Damages textbook with Dr. Rick Dunford at Environmental Economics Services, LLC, validating empirical frameworks and statistical model accuracy across hazardous-substance damages cases.

On campus, I serve as a Graduate Teaching Assistant for EC 490 (Senior Seminar) and EC 410 (Public Finance), and I am a graduate affiliate of the Center for Environmental and Resource Economic Policy (CEnREP). Through CEnREP I participate in the Triangle Resources and Environmental Economics (TREE) seminar — a joint Duke–NC State forum convening leading scholars in environmental valuation, natural resource economics, and energy policy.

My research interests center on the non-market valuation of environmental goods, spatial econometrics, and the economic consequences of environmental contamination on communities — with my thesis examining the marginal cost threshold at which proposed PFAS surface water regulations become welfare-improving for the North Carolina public.

Before returning to graduate school, I built a five-year career across alternative investments, expert-network research, data analytics, and federal policy — beginning with a Legislative Research Internship in the Washington office of Congressman Seth Moulton, where I tracked utility-recovery data following the 2018 Merrimack Valley gas explosions and shaped constituent communications across the 6th District of Massachusetts.

Out of college I joined Procession Systems, a three-year-old government-contracting startup in Reston, VA, where I built and ran the firm's data analytics function — surfacing recruiter performance trends, forecasting hiring demand, and supporting cleared-talent placement across federal contractors.

I subsequently moved to Third Bridge in New York, where I was promoted from Research Associate to Client Associate to lead a four-person team running 4–6 simultaneous due diligence engagements (3–5 week cycles) for private equity and management consulting clients including Bain Capital, Astorg, and Boston Consulting Group. I then joined Obra Capital in Austin to support investor relations across a $6.9B platform spanning Special Situations, Longevity, and Real Estate Credit products.

Most recently at Standish Management, I served as Client Associate in Investor Relations, leveraging Alteryx for financial modeling and performance analytics across 15+ investment funds — including TrueBridge Capital ($9.57B AUM) — and contributing to the firm's Anti-Money-Laundering team for investor background diligence. That applied, analytical foundation now informs the precision and rigor I bring to every research question I pursue in graduate school.

Thesis & Research

Thesis · In Progress

Valuing Regulatory Justification: A Tipping Point Analysis of PFAS Surface Water Quality Standards in North Carolina

This research moves beyond aggregate cost-benefit metrics to identify the marginal cost per household at which proposed PFAS surface water regulations become welfare-improving to the public bearing the burden. While the state's Regulatory Impact Analysis (RIA) indicates aggregate monetized benefits of approximately $11.67 billion outweigh $11.19 billion in costs, these totals mask the friction between diffuse, long-term health benefits and the concentrated, near-term costs realized by ratepayers and industrial dischargers. Employing contingent valuation and choice experiments cross-validated with revealed preference evidence from residential property markets, this thesis elicits willingness-to-pay (WTP) thresholds for specific regulatory outcomes. The resulting marginal cost threshold provides a politically and economically justifiable price point directly actionable for the North Carolina Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ) in regulatory proceedings and public engagement.

Working Paper · ECG 561 Applied Econometrics I

A Dam Catch-22.3: The Non-Linear Spatial Effects of North Carolina's Water Infrastructure on Housing Markets

Infrastructure projects generate complex spatial externalities that ripple through local housing markets in ways traditional methods often fail to capture. Using cross-sectional data across 747 Zip Code Tabulation Areas (ZCTAs) in North Carolina and geolocated data for over 3,600 dams, I employ a hedonic pricing model with quadratic functional forms to estimate the capitalization of dam proximity into residential property values. Standard OLS estimates yield modest relationships, but after correcting for the endogeneity of residential sorting via an Instrumental Variable (IV) strategy, I identify a statistically significant non-linear amenity gradient (p < 0.05). Dams act as a net amenity for approximately 22.3 miles before the price effect dissipates — a finding that challenges the arbitrary fixed-radius assumptions common in infrastructure policy analysis and cost-benefit assessment.

Research Tool · Open Access

Econometric Advisor — An AI-Powered Research Design Tool for Applied Economics

Launch app →

Building a defensible identification strategy is one of the earliest — and most consequential — decisions in any applied research project, yet many graduate students arrive at it without a structured way to think it through. The Econometric Advisor walks the researcher through a twelve-question diagnostic of their study design and, on the other side, returns a tailored toolkit: a recommended identification approach, a defensible estimator, functional-form guidance, spatial-dependence diagnostics, and an inference checklist calibrated to the project at hand. With an Anthropic API key supplied, the tool also generates a plain-English narrative of the research design alongside a curated review of relevant applied economics literature. The Advisor runs entirely in the browser as a static site — pure HTML and JavaScript on GitHub Pages — and reflects my interest in lowering the barrier to rigorous econometric thinking for first-time researchers navigating the identification landscape.
